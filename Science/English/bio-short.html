Biology - Short Answer Questions

Class 12 Bihar Board | Most Important 60 Questions | 2 Marks Each
Q1. Differentiate between In-situ and Ex-situ conservation.
In-situ Conservation: Endangered organisms are protected in their natural habitats (forests). Examples: National Parks, Sanctuaries.
Ex-situ Conservation: Organisms are taken out of their natural habitat and placed in man-made, protected environments. Examples: Zoos, Botanical Gardens, Gene Banks.
Q2. State the symptoms and cause of Down's Syndrome.
It is a genetic disorder caused by the presence of an extra copy of chromosome number 21 (Trisomy 21). The total number of chromosomes becomes 47.
Symptoms: Short stature, round head, Mongolian-like face, furrowed and protruding tongue, and retarded mental development.
Q3. Differentiate between Spermatogenesis and Oogenesis.
Spermatogenesis: The process of sperm formation in the Testis. It starts at puberty and continues throughout life.
Oogenesis: The process of ovum (egg) formation in the Ovary. It ceases around the age of 50 years (Menopause).
Q4. Write the pathogen and symptoms of Typhoid and Ringworm.
(1) Typhoid: Caused by the bacterium Salmonella typhi. Symptoms: Sustained high fever, stomach pain, and loss of appetite.
(2) Ringworm: Caused by fungi like Microsporum or Trichophyton. Symptoms: Dry, scaly, and itchy lesions on the skin.
Q5. What are Restriction Endonucleases?
These are special enzymes that cut DNA molecules at specific locations (Palindromic sequences). Hence, they are called 'Molecular Scissors'. They are used in genetic engineering to isolate desired genes. Example: EcoRI.
Q6. What is Double Fertilization and Triple Fusion?
In angiosperms, fertilization occurs twice. One male gamete fuses with the egg cell, called Syngamy (True Fertilization). The second male gamete fuses with two polar nuclei in the central cell, called Triple Fusion. These two events together are called Double Fertilization.
Q7. What are Biofertilizers? Give examples.
Microorganisms that naturally enrich the nutrient quality of the soil are called biofertilizers. Unlike chemical fertilizers, they do not cause pollution.
Examples: Rhizobium bacteria found in root nodules of legumes, and Cyanobacteria (Blue-green algae) in paddy fields.
Q8. Differentiate between DNA and RNA.
DNA: It is the main genetic material. It is double-stranded, contains Deoxyribose sugar and Thymine base.
RNA: It mainly participates in protein synthesis. It is single-stranded, contains Ribose sugar and Uracil base instead of Thymine.
Q9. What are Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs)? What is Bt Cotton?
Organisms (plants, bacteria, animals) whose genes have been altered by introducing a foreign gene through genetic engineering are called GMOs.
Example: Bt Cotton is a GMO containing a gene from the bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis, which makes it resistant to bollworm insects.
Q10. What are Vasectomy and Tubectomy?
These are permanent methods of family planning:
Vasectomy (Male sterilization): The vas deferens is cut and tied to prevent sperm from entering the semen.
Tubectomy (Female sterilization): The fallopian tube is cut and tied to prevent fertilization.
Q11. Differentiate between Food Chain and Food Web.
Food Chain: A straight, linear sequence of energy flow in an ecosystem (e.g., Grass → Deer → Lion).
Food Web: A complex network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem, which provides stability to the system.
Q12. What is the function of the Head of a human sperm?
The head contains a large haploid nucleus with genetic material. The anterior portion is covered by a cap-like structure called the 'Acrosome', which contains enzymes (hyaluronidase) that help penetrate the ovum during fertilization.
Q13. What is Biopiracy?
When multinational companies or organizations use biological resources (like plants, herbs) or traditional knowledge of a country without proper authorization or compensatory payment, it is called Biopiracy. Example: Patent controversies over Basmati rice and Turmeric.
Q14. Write two main applications of DNA Fingerprinting.
1. In Forensic Science: To accurately identify criminals from blood or hair samples found at a crime scene.
2. In Paternity Tests: To determine biological parents in case of disputes by checking genetic similarity.
Q15. Differentiate between Homologous and Analogous organs.
Homologous Organs: Same origin and structure but different functions (e.g., Human hand and Whale flipper). They show 'Divergent Evolution'.
Analogous Organs: Same function but different origin and structure (e.g., Wings of butterfly and bird). They show 'Convergent Evolution'.
Q16. What is Transcription?
The process of copying genetic information from one strand of DNA (template strand) into mRNA within the cell nucleus is called Transcription. This process is catalyzed by the enzyme RNA Polymerase. It is the first step of protein synthesis.
Q17. Explain Mutualism with an example.
It is a close relationship between two different species where both benefit from each other.
Example: Lichens, a symbiotic association between algae and fungi. Algae prepare food via photosynthesis, while fungi provide shelter and absorb water/minerals from the soil.
Q18. Write the causes and effects of Global Warming.
The rise in the Earth's average temperature due to increased greenhouse gases (like CO₂, Methane, CFCs) is called Global Warming.
Effects: Melting of polar ice caps, rising sea levels, and unpredictable climate changes (e.g., El Nino effect).
Q19. What is Parthenocarpy and Parthenogenesis?
Parthenocarpy: Formation of fruit from the ovary without fertilization (e.g., Banana). Such fruits are seedless.
Parthenogenesis: Development of a new organism directly from the female gamete without fertilization (e.g., Male drones in honeybees).
Q20. What is the difference between Nucleoside and Nucleotide?
Nucleoside: It is formed by the union of a nitrogenous base (Purine/Pyrimidine) and a pentose sugar (Base + Sugar).
Nucleotide: When a phosphate group attaches to a nucleoside, it becomes a nucleotide (Base + Sugar + Phosphate). It is the structural unit of DNA and RNA.
Q21. What is Tissue Culture?
The technique of growing a whole plant from a cell or tissue (Explant) on a nutrient medium under sterile and controlled conditions in a laboratory is called tissue culture. Thousands of plants can be produced in a short time via this method (Micropropagation).
Q22. Describe the structure of the Embryo Sac.
A typical mature angiosperm embryo sac is a 7-celled and 8-nucleate structure.
1. Micropylar end: Three cells (One Egg cell + Two Synergids).
2. Chalazal end: Three Antipodal cells.
3. Center: One large Central cell containing two polar nuclei.
Q23. Differentiate between Innate and Acquired Immunity.
Innate Immunity: Present from birth. It is non-specific and acts as a barrier to all pathogens (e.g., Skin, Stomach acid).
Acquired Immunity: Developed during one's lifetime. It is pathogen-specific and memory-based (e.g., Antibodies formed after vaccination or infection).
Q24. Write two harmful effects of Alcohol consumption.
1. Effect on Liver: Excessive alcohol damages the liver leading to a severe condition called 'Liver Cirrhosis'.
2. Effect on Nervous System: It depresses the central nervous system, impairing judgment, physical balance, and memory.
Q25. What is the nature of the Pyramid of Energy? Explain.
The pyramid of energy in an ecosystem is always upright. According to thermodynamic laws, energy is lost as heat when transferring from one trophic level to another. Only 10% of energy reaches the next level; thus, energy is maximum at the base and minimum at the top.
Q26. What is the cloning vector pBR322?
pBR322 is an artificial plasmid vector derived from E. coli bacteria. It is used in genetic engineering to transfer foreign DNA into a host cell. It contains two antibiotic resistance genes (Ampicillin and Tetracycline) which act as selectable markers.
Q27. What is Polyembryony?
The phenomenon of the occurrence of more than one embryo in a single seed is called Polyembryony. While common in Gymnosperms, in Angiosperms it is found in specific varieties like Orange, Lemon, and Mango (Citrus fruits).
Q28. Define Linkage.
The physical association of genes located on the same chromosome, causing them to be inherited together rather than separating during inheritance, is called Linkage. This is an exception to Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment. Discovered by Morgan in Drosophila.
Q29. Write two adaptations of Xerophytes (Desert plants).
Desert plants are adapted to conserve water:
1. Their leaves are modified into spines to reduce transpiration (e.g., Cactus).
2. Stems become fleshy, green, and photosynthetic (Phylloclade), and they possess sunken stomata.
Q30. What is Incomplete Dominance?
It is an exception to Mendel's laws. When a cross is made between two parents with contrasting traits, and the F1 generation shows neither dominant trait but an intermediate mixture, it is called Incomplete Dominance.
Example: Snapdragon (Pink flowers = Red + White).
Q31. What is Gel Electrophoresis?
It is a laboratory technique used to separate DNA, RNA, or protein molecules based on their size and charge. Since DNA is negatively charged, it moves towards the Anode (positive electrode) under an electric field.
Q32. What is Amniocentesis?
It is a prenatal diagnostic technique. A small amount of amniotic fluid is extracted from the uterus to analyze the chromosomal pattern of fetal cells. It detects genetic disorders like Down's syndrome. (Note: Its use for sex determination is banned).
Q33. What is an Antibody and how does it work?
An Antibody is a 'Y'-shaped protein (Immunoglobulin) produced by B-lymphocytes in response to foreign substances. When a pathogen (Antigen) enters the body, antibodies recognize and neutralize or destroy it.
Q34. What is a Test Tube Baby?
It is a method to treat infertility. The fertilization of the egg and sperm takes place outside the body in a laboratory (IVF - In Vitro Fertilization). The resulting early embryo is then transferred back into the woman's uterus for normal development.
Q35. What are the functions of the Placenta?
The placenta is the link between the mother and the fetus. Its main functions are:
1. Supply oxygen and nutrients from mother to fetus.
2. Remove waste products produced by the fetus.
3. Secrete hormones like Progesterone and hCG to maintain pregnancy.
Q36. What are the Start and Stop codons of the Genetic Code?
Start Codon: It is AUG. It initiates protein synthesis and also codes for the amino acid 'Methionine'.
Stop Codons: There are three: UAA, UAG, UGA. They do not code for any amino acid, causing protein synthesis to stop.
Q37. Differentiate between Active and Passive Immunity.
Active Immunity: Produced when the body creates its own antibodies upon exposure to antigens. Slow but long-lasting (e.g., Vaccination).
Passive Immunity: Produced when ready-made antibodies are introduced into the body. Fast acting but short-lived (e.g., IgA in mother's milk).
Q38. What is Haemophilia (Bleeder's Disease)?
It is a sex-linked (X-linked) recessive genetic disorder. The patient lacks a protein required for blood clotting. Consequently, even a minor cut results in non-stop bleeding, which can be fatal. It is more common in males.
Q39. What is the Ozone Hole and Dobson Unit?
The thinning of the ozone layer in the Stratosphere is called the 'Ozone Hole'. It is most prominent over Antarctica. The main cause is CFC gases. The thickness of the ozone layer is measured in Dobson Units (DU).
Q40. State adaptations for Wind Pollination.
Plants pollinated by wind have the following characteristics:
1. Pollen grains are light, dry, and produced in large numbers.
2. Stigma is large, sticky, and feathery to trap airborne pollen.
3. Flowers are small, not colorful, and lack nectar (e.g., Maize, Grass).
Q41. What is Point Mutation?
A mutation arising due to a change, insertion, or deletion of a single base pair in the DNA sequence is called a Point Mutation.
Example: Sickle Cell Anemia, where a change occurs in the 6th codon of the hemoglobin gene.
Q42. Describe the structure of Microsporangium.
A microsporangium is a cylindrical structure in the anther where pollen grains are formed. It is surrounded by four wall layers: (1) Epidermis, (2) Endothecium, (3) Middle layers, and (4) Tapetum. The innermost layer, 'Tapetum', provides nutrition to developing pollen.
Q43. What is the use of PCR?
PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) is a revolutionary technique used to amplify a specific DNA segment into billions of copies in a very short time in the lab. It is primarily used in diagnosing genetic diseases, DNA fingerprinting, and forensic investigations.
Q44. What are BOD and Flocs in sewage treatment?
BOD (Biological Oxygen Demand): It is the amount of oxygen consumed by bacteria to decompose organic matter in water. Higher BOD means more pollution.
Flocs: Masses of bacteria associated with fungal filaments to form mesh-like structures during secondary treatment are called Flocs.
Q45. What is Co-dominance? Give an example.
When two alleles present in an organism express themselves fully and simultaneously, it is called Co-dominance. Neither trait is suppressed.
Example: Human AB Blood group, where red blood cells have both Antigen A and Antigen B.
Q46. Write the benefits of Micropropagation.
Growing thousands of plants through tissue culture is called Micropropagation. Benefits:
1. A large number of plants can be produced in limited space and time.
2. The plants produced are genetically identical (Somaclones) and virus-free (disease-free).
Q47. Define Biodiversity Hotspot.
Regions on Earth with very high species richness and high endemism (species found nowhere else), which are facing a threat of extinction due to human activity, are called Biodiversity Hotspots. Example: Western Ghats in India.
Q48. Differentiate between Euchromatin and Heterochromatin.
Euchromatin: Loosely packed region of chromatin that stains light. It is genetically active (Transcription occurs here).
Heterochromatin: Densely packed region that stains dark. It is genetically inactive.
Q49. State the symptoms and causative agent of AIDS.
The causative agent is HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus), which destroys the body's immune system (T-lymphocytes).
Symptoms: The patient loses immunity, leading to frequent fever, diarrhea, weight loss, and susceptibility to infections like pneumonia.
Q50. What is Cancer and Metastasis?
A tumor formed by uncontrolled growth and division of cells is called cancer.
Metastasis: This is the most dreaded property of malignant tumors. Cancer cells detach from the original tumor, travel via blood to other parts of the body, and start new tumors there.
Q51. What are Sacred Groves?
In many tribal regions of India, patches of forests are protected and worshipped as sacred to deities. Cutting trees or harming animals here is strictly prohibited. It is a traditional method of biodiversity conservation. Example: Khasi and Jaintia Hills in Meghalaya.
Q52. What is the Founder Effect?
When a small group of individuals separates from a large population and establishes a new colony in a new geographical area, their genetic makeup differs from the original population. They become 'founders' of the new population. This phenomenon is called the Founder Effect (a form of Genetic Drift).
Q53. What is Downstream Processing?
In biotechnology, after a product (drug/protein) is formed in a bioreactor, it undergoes several processes before marketing. This includes Separation and Purification of the product. This entire sequence is called Downstream Processing.
Q54. What is the ELISA test?
ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay) is a diagnostic technique based on antigen-antibody interaction. It is primarily used for the early detection of AIDS (HIV infection). It detects infection using enzymes.
Q55. What is the MOET technique?
MOET (Multiple Ovulation Embryo Transfer) is a breed improvement method in cattle. A superior cow is given FSH-like hormones to produce 6-8 eggs (Superovulation). After artificial insemination, the fertilized embryos are transferred non-surgically to surrogate mothers.
Q56. What are Cannabinoids?
It is a group of chemicals that interact with receptors in the brain. They are obtained from the flowers, leaves, and resin of the Cannabis plant (Cannabis sativa).
Examples: Marijuana, Hashish, Charas, and Ganja. They are abused as drugs.
Q57. Explain the Competitive Exclusion Principle.
Proposed by Gause, it states that if two closely related species are competing for the same limited resource (e.g., food), they cannot co-exist indefinitely. The species that is inferior in competition will eventually be eliminated.
Q58. What is DNA Polymerase and its role?
It is the main enzyme involved in DNA replication. Its function is to read the old DNA strand (template) and add complementary nucleotides to build a new strand. Thus, it helps in creating two new DNA molecules from one old one with high speed and accuracy.
Q59. What is Pedigree Analysis?
The study and record of the inheritance of a specific trait or genetic disorder (like Haemophilia, Color blindness) across several generations of a family, represented by a chart, is called Pedigree Analysis. It helps in genetic counseling.
Q60. Differentiate between True Fruit and False Fruit.
True Fruit: Fruits that develop solely from the Ovary after fertilization. Examples: Mango, Grape.
False Fruit (Pseudocarp): Fruits in which other floral parts like the Thalamus also contribute to fruit formation along with the ovary. Examples: Apple, Strawberry, Cashew.
