Q1. What is Biodiversity? Explain the main causes of its loss and distinguish between In-situ and Ex-situ conservation.
(A) Definition: Biodiversity refers to the variety of life forms, including the genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity found on Earth.

(B) Causes of Biodiversity Loss (The Evil Quartet):
  1. Habitat Loss & Fragmentation: This is the primary cause. Deforestation destroys natural homes of species (e.g., Amazon rainforest destruction).
  2. Over-exploitation: Excessive use of natural resources by humans leads to extinction (e.g., Passenger Pigeon, Steller's Sea Cow).
  3. Alien Species Invasion: Introduction of non-native species threatens local species (e.g., Water Hyacinth, Parthenium).
  4. Co-extinction: When a host species becomes extinct, the parasites or other species dependent on it also become extinct.
(C) Difference in Conservation:
In-situ ConservationEx-situ Conservation
Conserving species within their natural habitat.Conserving species outside their natural habitat in special settings.
Examples: National Parks, Sanctuaries, Biosphere Reserves.Examples: Zoos, Botanical Gardens, Seed Banks, Cryopreservation.
Q2. Describe the process of Double Fertilization in flowering plants (Angiosperms) and explain the post-fertilization events.
Introduction: Double fertilization is a unique characteristic of Angiosperms discovered by Nawaschin. It involves two male gametes.
Process Steps:
  1. Pollen grains germinate on the stigma, forming a pollen tube that enters the ovule.
  2. The pollen tube releases two male gametes into the embryo sac.
  3. Syngamy (True Fertilization): One male gamete fuses with the egg cell.
    Male Gamete (n) + Egg Cell (n) → Zygote (2n)
  4. Triple Fusion: The second male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei in the central cell.
    Male Gamete (n) + 2 Polar Nuclei (n+n) → PEN (3n)
Post-fertilization Events:
  • Zygote: Develops into the Embryo.
  • Primary Endosperm Nucleus (PEN): Develops into the Endosperm (provides nutrition to the embryo).
  • Ovule: Hardens and converts into a Seed.
  • Ovary: Ripens to become the Fruit.
Q3. What is the Menstrual Cycle? Describe its various phases along with hormonal regulation.
Definition: The rhythmic series of changes in the reproductive system of female primates (monkeys, apes, humans) is called the menstrual cycle. It lasts about 28-29 days.
Phases and Hormonal Control:
  1. Menstrual Phase (Days 1-5): Due to the withdrawal of Progesterone (if fertilization fails), the endometrial lining of the uterus breaks down, causing bleeding.
  2. Follicular Phase (Days 6-13): Secretion of FSH and LH increases from the pituitary. This stimulates the development of primary follicles into Graafian follicles in the ovary. The growing follicles secrete Estrogen, which repairs the endometrium.
  3. Ovulatory Phase (Day 14): Both LH and FSH reach peak levels (LH Surge). This causes the rupture of the Graafian follicle and release of the ovum (Ovulation).
  4. Luteal Phase (Days 15-28): The remaining parts of the follicle transform into the Corpus Luteum. It secretes large amounts of Progesterone, essential for maintaining the endometrium for potential pregnancy. If no pregnancy occurs, it degenerates, starting a new cycle.
Q4. What is Genetic Engineering? Describe the steps of Recombinant DNA (rDNA) technology in detail.
Genetic Engineering: The technique of altering the genetic material (DNA/RNA) of an organism and introducing it into another organism to change its phenotype.
Steps of rDNA Technology:
  1. Isolation of Genetic Material (DNA): Breaking the cell wall using enzymes (lysozyme/cellulase) to extract pure DNA.
  2. Cutting DNA: Using Restriction Endonucleases (Molecular Scissors) to cut DNA at specific recognition sites.
  3. Amplification of Gene (PCR): Using Polymerase Chain Reaction to create millions of copies of the desired gene.
  4. Formation of rDNA: The desired gene is ligated (joined) with a Vector (e.g., Plasmid) using the enzyme DNA Ligase.
  5. Transformation: Introducing the rDNA into a host organism (like *E. coli*).
  6. Obtaining the Product: Culturing the host cells in bioreactors to produce the desired protein on a large scale.
Q5. What are Chromosomal Disorders? Describe the causes and symptoms of Down’s syndrome, Klinefelter’s syndrome, and Turner’s syndrome.
Definition: Disorders caused by the absence, excess, or abnormal arrangement of one or more chromosomes (Aneuploidy) due to failure of segregation during cell division.
Major Disorders:
  1. Down’s Syndrome (Trisomy 21):
    • Cause: Presence of an extra copy of chromosome 21. Total chromosomes = 47.
    • Symptoms: Short stature, round head, protruding tongue, palm crease, physical and mental retardation.
  2. Klinefelter’s Syndrome (XXY):
    • Cause: Presence of an extra X chromosome in males. Total = 47.
    • Symptoms: Sterile male, underdeveloped testes, development of female characters like breasts (Gynecomastia).
  3. Turner’s Syndrome (XO):
    • Cause: Absence of one X chromosome in females. Total = 45.
    • Symptoms: Sterile female, rudimentary ovaries, lack of secondary sexual characters.
Q6. Describe the applications of Biotechnology in Agriculture (Bt Cotton) and Medicine (Genetically Engineered Insulin).
(A) In Agriculture - Bt Cotton:
  • Principle: The soil bacterium *Bacillus thuringiensis* contains a specific 'cry gene' that produces a toxic protein.
  • Mechanism: This gene is inserted into the cotton plant. When an insect (like Bollworm) eats the plant, the toxin becomes active in the alkaline gut of the insect, causing lysis and death.
  • Benefit: Reduces reliance on chemical pesticides and increases yield.
(B) In Medicine - Genetically Engineered Insulin (Humulin):
  • Earlier, insulin was extracted from slaughtered cattle/pigs, causing allergies.
  • Method: DNA sequences corresponding to chains A and B of human insulin were introduced into the plasmids of *E. coli*.
  • The bacteria produced insulin chains separately, which were extracted and combined by disulfide bonds to form mature human insulin (**Humulin**).
Q7. Write an essay on Tissue Culture. Describe its types and the process of Micropropagation.
Tissue Culture: A technique to grow whole plants from any part of a plant (explant) in a sterile nutrient medium under controlled laboratory conditions.
Principle: It is based on **'Totipotency'**, the ability of a single plant cell to generate a whole plant.
Process:
  1. Explant: A small plant part (e.g., shoot tip) is taken and sterilized.
  2. Callus Formation: In the nutrient medium, cells divide to form an undifferentiated mass called 'Callus'.
  3. Organogenesis: By adjusting hormones (Auxins for roots, Cytokinins for shoots), roots and shoots are regenerated.
Micropropagation: The method of producing thousands of plants in a very short time using tissue culture.
Significance: Produces genetically identical plants (**Somaclones**) and virus-free plants (using Meristem culture).
Q8. Describe the different types of ecological pyramids (Number, Biomass, Energy) with suitable examples.
Ecological Pyramids: A graphical representation of the relationship between different trophic levels in terms of number, biomass, or energy.
  1. Pyramid of Number: Represents the number of individuals at each level.
    • Upright: Grassland ecosystem (Producers > Herbivores > Carnivores).
    • Inverted: Tree ecosystem (One tree supports many birds and thousands of parasites).
  2. Pyramid of Biomass: Represents the total dry weight of organisms.
    • Upright: Forest/Terrestrial ecosystem.
    • Inverted: Aquatic (Pond) ecosystem, as small standing crop of phytoplankton supports a large biomass of fish.
  3. Pyramid of Energy: Represents energy flow.
    It is **Always Upright**. According to Lindeman’s **10% Law**, only 10% of energy is transferred to the next trophic level, so energy always decreases.
Q9. Describe the pathogen, mode of transmission, symptoms, and prevention of AIDS.
Introduction: AIDS (Acquired Immuno Deficiency Syndrome) is a fatal disease that destroys the immune system.
Pathogen: Caused by **HIV** (Human Immunodeficiency Virus), a retrovirus containing RNA.
Modes of Transmission:
  1. Unsafe sexual contact with an infected person.
  2. Transfusion of contaminated blood or blood products.
  3. Sharing infected needles/syringes (common in drug abusers).
  4. From infected mother to child through placenta.
Symptoms: Destruction of helper T-lymphocytes leads to severe immunodeficiency. Symptoms include persistent fever, weight loss, diarrhea, and susceptibility to opportunistic infections (TB, Pneumonia).
Prevention:
  • Use of disposable needles.
  • Screening blood in blood banks.
  • Safe sex practices (use of condoms).
Q10. Describe the double-helical structure of DNA (Watson & Crick model) and explain the process of Transcription.
(A) DNA Structure (Watson & Crick, 1953):
  1. DNA consists of two poly-nucleotide strands coiled around a central axis to form a **Double Helix**.
  2. The strands are **Anti-parallel**; one runs 5'→3', the other 3'→5'.
  3. The backbone is made of sugar-phosphate, with nitrogenous bases projecting inwards.
  4. Base Pairing: Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) via **2 Hydrogen bonds**, and Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C) via **3 Hydrogen bonds**.
(B) Transcription: The process of copying genetic information from one strand of DNA into **mRNA**.
  • Site: Nucleus.
  • Enzyme: DNA-dependent **RNA Polymerase**.
  • Process: Occurs in three steps—Initiation, Elongation, and Termination. Only one DNA strand acts as a template. The RNA is synthesized in the 5'→3' direction.
Q11. Explain the mechanism of Sex Determination in Humans, Birds, and Honey Bees.
1. Humans (XX-XY Type):
  • Female is Homogametic (XX), producing only X-type eggs.
  • Male is Heterogametic (XY), producing 50% X-sperm and 50% Y-sperm.
  • Fertilization by Y-sperm produces a male (XY). Thus, **sex is determined by the father**.
2. Birds (ZZ-ZW Type):
  • Female is Heterogametic (ZW), producing two types of eggs.
  • Male is Homogametic (ZZ).
  • Here, **sex is determined by the female**.
3. Honey Bees (Haplo-diploid Mechanism):
  • Sex is determined by the number of chromosome sets.
  • Fertilized eggs (Diploid, 2n=32) develop into **Females** (Queen/Worker).
  • Unfertilized eggs (Haploid, n=16) develop into **Males (Drones)** via **Parthenogenesis**.
Q12. What is the Immune System? Differentiate between Active and Passive Immunity and write a note on Vaccination.
Immunity: The ability of the body to recognize and fight pathogens (bacteria, viruses) mediated by B and T lymphocytes.
Difference:

Upon actual infection later, these memory cells recognize the pathogen and launch a massive attack to eliminate it.
Q13. How are microbes useful in Sewage Treatment and Biogas Production?
(A) In Sewage Treatment: Urban wastewater (sewage) is treated before discharge.
  1. Primary Treatment: Physical removal of large particles.
  2. Secondary (Biological) Treatment: Uses aerobic microbes. Bacteria and fungi form 'Flocs' that consume organic matter, significantly reducing the **BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand)** of the water, making it less polluting.
(B) In Biogas Production:
  • Biogas (mainly Methane) is produced by anaerobic bacteria called **Methanogens** (e.g., *Methanobacterium*).
  • These bacteria grow on cellulosic material (like cow dung) in the absence of oxygen.
  • They break down cellulose to produce a mixture of gases (CH4 + CO2 + H2), which serves as excellent fuel.
Q14. Describe the process of Spermatogenesis with a diagram and explain the significance of the male reproductive system.
Spermatogenesis: The process of formation of sperms from immature germ cells in the seminiferous tubules of the testis. Initiated by **GnRH** at puberty.
Steps:
  1. Multiplication Phase: Spermatogonia (2n) multiply by mitosis.
  2. Growth Phase: Some develop into **Primary Spermatocytes (2n)**.
  3. Maturation Phase: Primary spermatocyte undergoes **Meiosis I** to form two haploid 'Secondary Spermatocytes' (n). These undergo **Meiosis II** to form four haploid **'Spermatids'**.
  4. Spermiogenesis: Non-motile spermatids transform into motile **Spermatozoa (Sperms)** by developing a tail.
Significance: Ensures the production of haploid male gametes for fertilization. The reproductive system facilitates transfer of sperms to the female tract.
Q15. Define Linkage and Crossing Over. Explain the significance of Crossing Over.
(A) Linkage: Discovered by T.H. Morgan in Drosophila. It is the tendency of genes located on the same chromosome to be inherited together.
  • Linked genes do not show Independent Assortment.
  • Result: Higher frequency of parental combinations and fewer recombinants in offspring.
(B) Crossing Over: The exchange of genetic material between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes during the **Pachytene** stage of Prophase-I of Meiosis.
Significance:
  • It breaks established linkage groups.
  • It introduces **New Recombinations** of genes.
  • It leads to **Genetic Variation**, which is the raw material for evolution.
Q16. Discuss the various methods of Contraception for population control.
Contraceptives are methods used to prevent unwanted pregnancies.
Major Methods:
  1. Natural Methods: Periodic abstinence (avoiding sex during fertile period, days 10-17) and Coitus Interruptus.
  2. Barrier Methods: Condoms, Diaphragms. They physically prevent the meeting of sperm and ovum. Condoms also protect against STDs.
  3. Intra Uterine Devices (IUDs): Inserted in the uterus (e.g., Copper-T). Copper ions suppress sperm motility and fertilizing capacity.
  4. Oral Contraceptives (Pills): 'Saheli' or 'Mala-D'. Contain hormones (Progesterone/Estrogen) that inhibit ovulation.
  5. Surgical Methods (Sterilization): Permanent method. **Vasectomy** in males (cutting vas deferens) and **Tubectomy** in females (cutting fallopian tube).
Q17. What is Pollution? Describe the causes and effects of Air Pollution and its control measures.
Pollution: Any undesirable change in physical, chemical, or biological characteristics of the environment.
Causes of Air Pollution: Vehicular exhaust, industrial emissions (SO2, CO), particulate matter (dust), and burning of crop residues.
Effects:
  1. Health: Respiratory disorders (Asthma, Bronchitis), lung cancer.
  2. Environment: **Acid Rain** (damages monuments like Taj Mahal) and **Global Warming** (Greenhouse effect).
Control Measures:
  • Use of unleaded petrol and **Catalytic Converters** in vehicles.
  • Use of **Electrostatic Precipitators** and Scrubbers in industries to filter smoke.
  • Afforestation (planting more trees).
Q18. Discuss Mendel’s Dihybrid Cross and explain the Law of Independent Assortment.
Dihybrid Cross: A cross involving two pairs of contrasting characters (e.g., Seed Shape and Seed Color).
Experiment: Mendel crossed Round-Yellow seeds (RRYY) with Wrinkled-Green seeds (rryy).
  • F1 Generation: All plants were Round-Yellow (RrYy) due to dominance.
  • F2 Generation: On selfing F1, four phenotypes were obtained in the ratio:
    Round-Yellow : Round-Green : Wrinkled-Yellow : Wrinkled-Green = 9 : 3 : 3 : 1
Law of Independent Assortment: The cross showed that "inheritance of one character is independent of the other." The factors (genes) for seed shape and color segregate independently during gamete formation, leading to the formation of new combinations (like Round-Green).
Q19. Discuss the principle, steps, and applications of DNA Fingerprinting.
Principle: Although 99.9% human DNA is identical, 0.1% varies. This region contains repetitive sequences called **VNTRs** (Variable Number of Tandem Repeats), which are unique to every individual.
Steps:
  1. Isolation of DNA from cells (blood, hair, semen).
  2. Digestion of DNA by Restriction Enzymes.
  3. Separation of fragments by **Gel Electrophoresis**.
  4. Transferring DNA to a nylon membrane (**Southern Blotting**).
  5. Hybridization using radioactive probes.
  6. Detection of bands on X-ray film (Autoradiography).
Applications: Forensic science (identifying criminals), settling paternity disputes, and studying evolutionary relationships.
Q20. Write an essay on Darwinism and explain Homologous and Analogous organs with examples.
Darwinism (Theory of Natural Selection): Charles Darwin, in *"Origin of Species"*, proposed:
  1. Overproduction: Organisms produce more offspring than can survive.
  2. Struggle for Existence: Competition for limited resources (food, space).
  3. Variations: No two individuals are alike. Beneficial variations help in survival.
  4. Natural Selection (Survival of the Fittest): Nature selects those best adapted to the environment. Over generations, this leads to the formation of new species.
Organs Comparison:
  • Homologous Organs: Same origin/structure, different function. (e.g., Forelimbs of Human, Cheetah, Bat). Show **Divergent Evolution**.
  • Analogous Organs: Different origin, same function. (e.g., Wings of Butterfly and Bird). Show **Convergent Evolution**.
