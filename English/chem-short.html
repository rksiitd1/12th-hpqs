Chemistry - Short Q&A

Class 12 Bihar Board | Top 60 Questions | 2 Marks Each
Q1. Write the IUPAC names of the following coordination compounds: K4[Fe(CN)6], [Ni(CO)4], and [Co(NH3)6]Cl3.
The IUPAC names of the given complex compounds are:
  • K4[Fe(CN)6]: Potassium hexacyanoferrate(II). The oxidation state of Iron is +2.
  • [Ni(CO)4]: Tetracarbonylnickel(0). The oxidation state of Nickel is 0.
  • [Co(NH3)6]Cl3: Hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride. The oxidation state of Cobalt is +3.
Q2. What is the difference between Schottky defect and Frenkel defect?
Schottky Defect: Equal numbers of cations and anions are missing from the crystal lattice, decreasing the density of the crystal (e.g., NaCl, KCl).
Frenkel Defect: An ion (usually cation) leaves its normal site and occupies an interstitial site. The density remains unchanged (e.g., AgCl, ZnS).
Q3. Differentiate between Calcination and Roasting.
Calcination: Heating the ore below its melting point in the absence or limited supply of air. It is used for carbonate and hydrated oxide ores.
Roasting: Heating the ore below its melting point in the presence of excess air. It is mainly used for sulphide ores.
Q4. Differentiate between Molarity and Molality.
Molarity (M): Number of moles of solute dissolved per liter of solution. It depends on volume, so it changes with temperature.
Molality (m): Number of moles of solute dissolved per kilogram of solvent. It involves mass, so it does not change with temperature.
Q5. Why do Transition Elements form colored ions and act as good catalysts?
Colored Ions: Due to the presence of incomplete (n-1)d orbitals. Electrons absorb energy and jump between d-orbitals (d-d transition), emitting complementary colors.
Catalytic Property: They have variable oxidation states and vacant d-orbitals, providing a large surface area for reactants to adsorb and react.
Q6. Why is the valency of inert gases zero?
Inert gases (like He, Ne, Ar) have a completely filled valence shell (stable octet configuration, ns2np6). They neither lose nor gain nor share electrons under normal conditions. Therefore, their combining capacity or valency is zero.
Q7. Define Brownian movement and Tyndall effect.
Brownian Movement: The continuous, zig-zag motion of colloidal particles in a dispersion medium caused by collisions with the molecules of the medium.
Tyndall Effect: The scattering of a beam of light by colloidal particles, making the path of the light visible when passed through a colloidal solution.
Q8. Define Osmotic pressure and Reverse Osmosis (RO).
Osmotic Pressure: The minimum external pressure that must be applied to the solution side to stop the flow of solvent into the solution across a semi-permeable membrane.
Reverse Osmosis: If a pressure greater than the osmotic pressure is applied to the solution, the solvent flows from the solution to the pure solvent side.
Q9. Explain why HF is a liquid while HCl is a gas.
Due to the small size and high electronegativity of Fluorine, HF molecules associate through strong intermolecular hydrogen bonding, existing as a liquid. In HCl, Chlorine is larger and less electronegative, so no H-bonding occurs; molecules are held by weak Van der Waals forces, making it a gas.
Q10. Why is Aniline less basic than Methylamine?
In Aniline (C6H5NH2), the lone pair of electrons on Nitrogen is delocalized over the benzene ring due to resonance. Thus, it is less available for donation to a proton. In Methylamine (CH3NH2), the +I effect increases electron density on Nitrogen, making it more basic.
Q11. State Faraday’s laws of electrolysis.
First Law: The mass of substance deposited or liberated at an electrode (W) is directly proportional to the quantity of charge (Q) passed. (W = ZIt).
Second Law: When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the masses of substances deposited are proportional to their chemical equivalent weights (E).
Q12. Write the difference between Order and Molecularity of a reaction.
Order: Sum of powers of concentration terms in the rate law. It is an experimental value and can be zero or fractional.
Molecularity: The number of reacting species colliding simultaneously in an elementary reaction. It is a theoretical concept and is always a whole number, never zero.
Q13. What is the effect of dilution on Molar conductivity and Specific conductivity?
Upon dilution, Molar conductivity (Λm) increases because the degree of dissociation increases and ionic mobility improves. However, Specific conductivity (κ) decreases because the number of current-carrying ions per unit volume decreases.
Q14. State Raoult’s Law for non-volatile solutes.
It states that for a solution of non-volatile solute, the relative lowering of vapor pressure (P0 - P) / P0 is equal to the mole fraction of the solute (xsolute) at a given temperature. This applies strictly to ideal solutions.
Q15. Write the names and formulas of important ores of Iron, Copper, Aluminium, and Zinc.
  • Iron: Haematite (Fe2O3)
  • Copper: Copper Pyrite (CuFeS2)
  • Aluminium: Bauxite (Al2O3ċ2H2O)
  • Zinc: Zinc Blende (ZnS)
Q16. What are the main differences between Physisorption and Chemisorption?
Physisorption: Arises due to weak Van der Waals forces. It is reversible, multi-layered, and favored at low temperatures.
Chemisorption: Arises due to strong chemical bonds. It is irreversible, mono-layered, and requires high activation energy (favored at higher temperatures initially).
Q17. Name the monomers of: Nylon-6,6, Teflon, and Buna-S.
  • Nylon-6,6: Hexamethylene diamine + Adipic acid.
  • Teflon: Tetrafluoroethene (CF2=CF2).
  • Buna-S: 1,3-Butadiene + Styrene.
Q18. Write the equation for the Carbylamine reaction.
When a primary amine (aliphatic or aromatic) is heated with chloroform and alcoholic KOH, an extremely foul-smelling substance called Isocyanide (Carbylamine) is formed.
R-NH2 + CHCl3 + 3KOH → R-NC + 3KCl + 3H2O
Q19. Write two main differences between DNA and RNA.
1. Sugar: DNA contains Deoxyribose sugar, whereas RNA contains Ribose sugar.
2. Base: DNA contains Thymine (T) as a base, whereas RNA contains Uracil (U) instead of Thymine. DNA is double-stranded; RNA is single-stranded.
Q20. Differentiate between Crystalline and Amorphous solids.
Crystalline: They have a regular, long-range arrangement of particles and a sharp melting point (e.g., Diamond, NaCl).
Amorphous: They have an irregular arrangement of particles and melt over a range of temperatures. Also known as supercooled liquids (e.g., Glass, Rubber).
Q21. What is the Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)?
It is a reference electrode whose potential is arbitrarily assigned as 0.00 V. It consists of a platinum foil coated with platinum black, dipped in a 1M acidic solution (H+), with pure Hydrogen gas bubbled through it at 1 atm pressure.
Q22. Why is Phenol more acidic than Alcohol?
After losing a proton (H+), Phenol forms a Phenoxide ion which is stabilized by resonance (delocalization of negative charge over the benzene ring). The Alkoxide ion formed from alcohol is not stabilized by resonance and is destabilized by the +I effect of the alkyl group.
Q23. Why is the bond angle in NH3 larger than in PH3?
Nitrogen is smaller and more electronegative than Phosphorus. This pulls the bond pairs closer to the Nitrogen atom, causing greater repulsion between them, resulting in a bond angle of 107°. In PH3, electron density is lower near P, leading to less repulsion (~93°).
Q24. Name the diseases caused by the deficiency of Vitamin A, C, and D.
  • Vitamin A: Night Blindness and Xerophthalmia (hardening of cornea).
  • Vitamin C: Scurvy (bleeding gums).
  • Vitamin D: Rickets (bone deformation in children) and Osteomalacia in adults.
Q25. Prove that the half-life of a first-order reaction is independent of initial concentration.
For a first-order reaction, the integrated rate equation is k = (2.303 / t) log([R]0 / [R]).
At half-life (t1/2), [R] = [R]0/2.
Substituting this: t1/2 = (2.303 / k) log(2) = 0.693 / k.
The formula does not contain the concentration term [R]0.
Q26. What is Activation Energy? Write the Arrhenius equation.
Activation Energy (Ea): The minimum extra amount of energy required by reactant molecules to cross the energy barrier and form products.
Arrhenius Equation: k = A e-Ea/RT, where k is the rate constant and A is the frequency factor.
Q27. Differentiate between Soap and Detergent.
Soap: Sodium or potassium salts of long-chain fatty acids. They do not lather in hard water due to the formation of insoluble calcium/magnesium precipitates.
Detergent: Sodium salts of sulfonic acids. They work well even in hard water and produce foam easily.
Q28. What is Lanthanoid Contraction?
The steady decrease in the atomic and ionic radii of lanthanoid elements with increasing atomic number is called Lanthanoid contraction. This is due to the poor shielding effect of 4f electrons, which allows the effective nuclear charge to pull the outer shell closer.
Q29. Differentiate between Double Salt and Complex Salt.
Double Salt: Stable in solid state but dissociates into its constituent ions in solution (loses identity). E.g., Mohr's Salt.
Complex Salt: Retains its identity in solid as well as in solution state. The complex ion does not dissociate. E.g., K4[Fe(CN)6].
Q30. Calculate the Effective Atomic Number (EAN) of Fe in [Fe(CN)6]4-.
Atomic number (Z) of Fe = 26. Oxidation state = +2. Ligands = 6 (CN- donates 2 electrons each).
Formula: EAN = Z - O.S. + 2 × (Coordination Number)
Calculation: EAN = 26 - 2 + (2 × 6) = 24 + 12 = 36 (Same as Krypton).
Q31. Define Gangue and Slag.
Gangue: The earthy impurities (sand, clay, rock) associated with the ore are called gangue or matrix.
Slag: The fusible product formed when flux reacts with gangue during the extraction of metals is called slag. (Flux + Gangue = Slag).
Q32. How is Ethanol converted to Ethylamine?
When a mixture of Ethanol (C2H5OH) and Ammonia (NH3) is passed over heated Alumina (Al2O3) at 360°C, Ethylamine is produced along with water.
C2H5OH + NH3 → C2H5NH2 + H2O
Q33. Differentiate between Thermoplastic and Thermosetting polymers.
Thermoplastic: Linear polymers that soften on heating and can be remolded repeatedly (e.g., Polythene, PVC).
Thermosetting: Cross-linked polymers that become hard and infusible on heating and cannot be remolded (e.g., Bakelite, Melamine).
Q34. Differentiate between Lyophilic and Lyophobic colloids.
Lyophilic (Solvent-loving): Strong affinity between dispersed phase and medium. They are reversible and very stable (e.g., Gum, Starch).
Lyophobic (Solvent-hating): No affinity between phase and medium. They are irreversible and require stabilizing agents (e.g., Gold sol, Metal sulphides).
Q35. Why are Interhalogen compounds more reactive than Halogens?
In interhalogen compounds (X-X'), the bond between two different halogens is polar and weaker than the bond in pure halogen molecules (X-X), except for F-F. The weaker bond breaks easily, making interhalogens more reactive.
Q36. Describe the structure of XeF4.
In XeF4, Xenon undergoes sp3d2 hybridization. It has 4 bond pairs and 2 lone pairs of electrons. To minimize repulsion, the lone pairs occupy axial positions. Thus, the shape of the molecule is Square Planar.
Q37. Define Analgesics and Antibiotics.
Analgesics: Chemical substances used to relieve pain without causing unconsciousness (e.g., Aspirin, Paracetamol).
Antibiotics: Substances produced by microorganisms that inhibit the growth of or kill other harmful microorganisms (e.g., Penicillin, Tetracycline).
Q38. What is the Cannizzaro reaction?
Aldehydes which do not have an α-hydrogen atom (e.g., HCHO, Benzaldehyde), when treated with concentrated alkali (NaOH/KOH), undergo self-oxidation and reduction (disproportionation). One molecule forms an alcohol, and another forms a carboxylic acid salt.
Q39. How will you distinguish between Methanol and Ethanol?
By Iodoform Test: Ethanol (C2H5OH) contains the CH3-CH(OH)- group. When heated with Iodine and NaOH, it gives a yellow precipitate of Iodoform (CHI3). Methanol does not contain this group and does not give this test.
Q40. Differentiate between Primary and Secondary cells.
Primary Cell: The reaction occurs only once, and the cell becomes dead after use. It cannot be recharged (e.g., Dry Cell).
Secondary Cell: It can be recharged by passing current in the opposite direction and can be used repeatedly (e.g., Lead Storage Battery).
Q41. What are Isotonic solutions?
Two solutions having the same osmotic pressure at a given temperature are called isotonic solutions. When separated by a semi-permeable membrane, no net flow of solvent occurs between them because their molar concentrations are effectively the same.
Q42. What is a Pseudo first-order reaction?
A chemical reaction in which the molecularity is 2 or more but the order of reaction is 1 is called a pseudo first-order reaction. This happens when one of the reactants (usually solvent like water) is present in large excess. E.g., Acid hydrolysis of ethyl acetate.
Q43. Define Ligand and Chelate.
Ligand: An ion or molecule capable of donating a pair of electrons to the central metal atom to form a coordinate bond.
Chelate: A ring structure formed when a polydentate ligand binds to a central metal atom through two or more donor sites.
Q44. What is the number of atoms (Z) in BCC and FCC unit cells?
BCC (Body Centered Cubic): Atoms at 8 corners and 1 at body center. Total = (8 × 1/8) + 1 = 2 atoms.
FCC (Face Centered Cubic): Atoms at 8 corners and 6 face centers. Total = (8 × 1/8) + (6 × 1/2) = 1 + 3 = 4 atoms.
Q45. Why is Formic acid stronger than Acetic acid?
Acetic acid (CH3COOH) contains a methyl group which has a +I (electron-releasing) effect. This destabilizes the carboxylate ion and strengthens the O-H bond, making proton release difficult. Formic acid (HCOOH) lacks this group, making it a stronger acid.
Q46. Write the equation for the Wurtz reaction.
When alkyl halides react with metallic sodium in the presence of dry ether, alkanes with double the number of carbon atoms are formed.
2R-X + 2Na → R-R + 2NaX
Q47. Give an example of a Food Preservative.
Food preservatives are chemicals that prevent food from spoilage due to microbial growth. Example: Sodium Benzoate (C6H5COONa). It is widely used in jams, pickles, and carbonated drinks.
Q48. Explain the mechanism of Rusting of Iron.
Rusting is an electrochemical process. The iron surface acts as an anode ($Fe \to Fe^{2+}$). Electrons move to a cathodic spot where Oxygen is reduced in the presence of $H^+$. The ferrous ions oxidize to form hydrated ferric oxide ($Fe_2O_3 \cdot xH_2O$), known as rust.
Q49. What are Enzymes?
Enzymes are complex nitrogenous organic compounds (globular proteins) produced in living cells. They act as **biological catalysts**, increasing the rate of biochemical reactions in living organisms. They are highly specific and work best at optimum temperature (37°C).
Q50. Write one main difference between SN1 and SN2 reactions.
SN1: Proceed in two steps involving a carbocation intermediate. The product is a racemic mixture (retention + inversion).
SN2: Proceed in a single step via a transition state. The product shows complete stereochemical inversion (Walden Inversion).
Q51. What is Power Alcohol?
A mixture of petrol and refined ethanol is called power alcohol. It typically contains 80% petrol and 20% ethanol (along with benzene). It is used as a fuel for internal combustion engines in automobiles to save fossil fuels.
Q52. Why is Cryolite added to Alumina during electrolysis?
Pure Alumina (Al2O3) has a very high melting point (~2050°C) and is a poor conductor. Adding Cryolite (Na3AlF6):
1. Lowers the melting point of the mix to ~900°C.
2. Increases the electrical conductivity of the molten electrolyte.
Q53. Write the formula for atomic radius in BCC and FCC unit cells.
If 'a' is the edge length of the unit cell:
BCC: Radius r = (√3 × a) / 4
FCC: Radius r = a / (2√2)
Q54. What is Peptization?
The process of converting a freshly prepared precipitate into a colloidal sol by shaking it with the dispersion medium in the presence of a small amount of electrolyte (peptizing agent) is called peptization.
Q55. What is the use of Fehling's solution and Tollens' reagent?
They are used to distinguish **Aldehydes** from Ketones. Aldehydes reduce Fehling's solution to a red precipitate ($Cu_2O$) and Tollens' reagent to metallic silver (Silver Mirror). Ketones generally do not respond to these tests.
Q56. What is the stability order of Carbocations?
A carbocation is an organic ion carrying a positive charge on a carbon atom. Due to the inductive effect (+I) and hyperconjugation of alkyl groups, the stability order is:
3° (Tertiary) > 2° (Secondary) > 1° (Primary) > Methyl.
Q57. Why is the Electron Affinity of Chlorine greater than Fluorine?
Fluorine has a very small atomic size, leading to high electron density in its 2p subshell. An incoming electron experiences strong repulsion. Chlorine has a larger size (3p subshell), so it can accommodate the extra electron more easily with less repulsion.
Q58. What are Biodegradable Polymers?
Polymers that can be decomposed by microorganisms (bacteria) over a period of time are called biodegradable polymers. They do not cause environmental pollution. Examples: PHBV and Nylon-2-nylon-6.
Q59. What are the reagents used for the Nitration of Benzene?
Nitration is carried out using a "Nitrating Mixture," which consists of concentrated Nitric Acid (HNO3) and concentrated Sulfuric Acid (H2SO4). The sulfuric acid acts as a catalyst to generate the electrophile, **Nitronium ion (NO2+)**.
Q60. Give examples of Network Solids.
Also known as Covalent Solids, these contain atoms linked by covalent bonds throughout the crystal, forming a giant network. They are very hard and have high melting points. Examples: Diamond (C), Silicon Carbide (SiC), and Quartz (SiO2).
